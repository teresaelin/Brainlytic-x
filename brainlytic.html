Knowledge Base

Core Concepts

Key neuropsychology ideas, organized by theme. Each entry is written to be clear, accurate, and thought-provoking.

Memory
Long-Term Potentiation (LTP)

The cellular mechanism thought to underlie learning and memory. When two neurons fire together repeatedly, the synapse between them strengthens — a biological expression of Hebb's rule: "neurons that fire together, wire together."

Memory
Working Memory

A short-term cognitive system that temporarily holds and manipulates information. Baddeley's model describes a central executive, phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, and episodic buffer — each serving a distinct role in active thought.

Cognition
Cognitive Load Theory

Sweller's framework proposes that learning is constrained by the limits of working memory. Intrinsic, extraneous, and germane loads shape how efficiently new schemas are built — with profound implications for education and interface design.

Cognition
Executive Function

An umbrella term for top-down regulatory processes: planning, cognitive flexibility, inhibitory control, and working memory updating. Largely governed by the prefrontal cortex, these functions distinguish deliberate thought from reactive behaviour.

Emotion
The Amygdala's Role in Fear

The amygdala acts as a threat-detection hub, initiating the fear response before conscious awareness. LeDoux's "low road" describes a fast, subcortical pathway to fear — bypassing the cortex entirely — explaining why fear can feel involuntary.

Emotion
Emotional Regulation

The ability to modulate emotional experience and expression. Strategies include cognitive reappraisal (reframing meaning) and suppression (hiding response), with distinct neural signatures and very different effects on psychological wellbeing.

Behaviour
Operant Conditioning

Skinner's model of how consequences shape behaviour. Reinforcement increases the likelihood of a behaviour; punishment decreases it. Variable-ratio reinforcement schedules — like social media likes — produce the most resistant-to-extinction behaviour.

Behaviour
The Default Mode Network

Active during rest and self-referential thought — daydreaming, mental time travel, and imagining others' perspectives. Disruption to this network is implicated in depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, and even creativity research.

Brain Regions
The Prefrontal Cortex

The seat of the "rational self." The PFC is responsible for planning, decision-making, social behaviour, and personality. Its prolonged maturation — not fully developed until the mid-20s — partially explains adolescent risk-taking.

Brain Regions
The Hippocampus

Critical for forming new declarative memories and spatial navigation. H.M.'s case — a patient who lost both hippocampi — revealed the distinction between explicit and implicit memory systems, reshaping the field entirely.

Disorders
Depression & Neuroplasticity

Major depressive disorder is associated with reduced hippocampal volume and impaired neurogenesis. Antidepressants may work partly by restoring neuroplasticity — a finding that reframes depression as a disorder of the brain's capacity to adapt.

Disorders
ADHD & Dopaminergic Pathways

ADHD involves dysregulation of dopamine in prefrontal circuits. The condition is better understood not as a deficit of attention, but as difficulty with selective attention — particularly when a task lacks immediate reward or novelty.

Questions & Answers

Answers to the big questions

Common myths, student questions, and deeper explanations — grouped by topic and answered with care.

Do we really only use 10% of our brains?
+

No — this is one of the most persistent myths in popular psychology. Brain imaging studies clearly show that we use virtually all parts of the brain, and that most of the brain is active almost all the time. The myth likely arose from misinterpretations of early neuroscience findings. In reality, the brain is metabolically expensive: it consumes roughly 20% of the body's energy despite being only 2% of its mass.

Are people truly "left-brained" or "right-brained"?
+

Not in the way popular culture suggests. While some functions are lateralised — language is predominantly left-hemisphere in most people — a large-scale neuroimaging study (Nielsen et al., 2013) found no evidence that individuals preferentially use one hemisphere over the other. Personality traits like creativity or logic cannot be meaningfully attributed to a single hemisphere.

Why do we forget things so quickly?
+

Forgetting is not a failure — it's an adaptive feature. Ebbinghaus's "forgetting curve" showed that memory decays rapidly without reinforcement, but spaced repetition dramatically slows this curve. The brain prioritises information that is emotionally significant, frequently accessed, or associated with strong contextual cues. Much of what we "forget" was never encoded into long-term memory in the first place.

Can memories ever be truly false?
+

Yes. Elizabeth Loftus's decades of research demonstrated that memory is reconstructive, not reproductive — it is rebuilt each time we recall it, making it susceptible to suggestion, distortion, and fabrication. In experiments, participants were reliably implanted with detailed false memories of events that never occurred. This has critical implications for eyewitness testimony and therapeutic practice.

Does the brain stop developing at age 18?
+

No. The prefrontal cortex — responsible for impulse control, planning, and judgement — continues maturing into the mid-20s. Myelination (the insulation of neural pathways that speeds processing) continues well beyond adolescence. Some research suggests that certain social-cognitive capacities continue developing into the 30s.

What is neuroplasticity, and is it limitless?
+

Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to reorganise its structure and function in response to experience. It is remarkable but not limitless. Plasticity is highest in early childhood — a sensitive period for language, sensory calibration, and social development. In adulthood, the brain retains plasticity through learning and recovery from injury, but change requires sustained effort and is constrained by existing architecture.

Is mental illness caused by a "chemical imbalance"?
+

The "chemical imbalance" theory — particularly the serotonin-deficiency model of depression — has been largely abandoned by researchers. Mental illness is now understood as arising from complex interactions between genetics, neurodevelopment, stress, trauma, and environment. The simplicity of the chemical imbalance narrative was useful for reducing stigma, but it was always an oversimplification that does not reflect current neuroscience.

Does talking about problems actually help, neurologically?
+

Yes, and the evidence is compelling. Labelling emotions — what researchers call "affect labelling" — reduces amygdala activation, essentially dampening the emotional alarm system. Psychotherapy has been shown to produce measurable changes in brain activity patterns comparable to pharmacological treatment in some conditions. Narrative and language appear to give the prefrontal cortex a handle on limbic arousal.

Self-Run Experiments

Experiment 001 · Cognitive Psychology
The Stroop Effect Under Emotional Arousal
Complete

Does emotional arousal amplify or diminish the Stroop interference effect? This experiment tests whether exposure to affective stimuli prior to a Stroop task modifies response times and error rates.

Aim

To assess whether induced emotional states alter Stroop task interference compared to a neutral baseline.

Hypothesis

Negative emotional arousal will increase Stroop interference (slower RT, more errors) by increasing attentional narrowing.

Method

N=24 self-selected participants; within-subjects design; IAPS images used for arousal induction; computerised Stroop task.

Results

Mean RT increased by 11% in the negative-arousal condition. Error rate rose from 4.2% to 9.8%. Effect size: d=0.64.

Conclusion

Partial support for the hypothesis. Results are consistent with attentional narrowing models, though confounds remain.

Experiment 002 · Memory
Spacing Effects in Self-Study: Does the Brain Know Best?
Complete

A personal longitudinal study testing whether self-administered spaced repetition outperforms massed practice on retention of neuropsychology terminology over a six-week period.

Aim

To replicate the spacing effect in a naturalistic personal learning context using vocabulary from a neuropsychology textbook.

Hypothesis

Spaced repetition will produce significantly higher retention at 6-week test compared to equivalent massed study time.

Method

Single-subject design; 80 terms; half studied via Anki (SRS), half via blocked study sessions. Blind testing at intervals.

Results

SRS condition: 78% retention at 6 weeks. Massed condition: 41% retention. Difference consistent across test intervals.

Conclusion

Strong replication of the spacing effect. Findings motivate broader adoption of SRS for academic study in memory-heavy fields.

Experiment 003 · Attention
Phone Proximity and Cognitive Capacity: A Desk Study
Ongoing

Inspired by Ward et al. (2017), this experiment investigates whether the mere presence of a smartphone on a desk reduces available working memory capacity, even when the phone is silent and face-down.

Aim

To test the "brain drain" hypothesis — that smartphone salience consumes attentional resources even without active use.

Hypothesis

Participants with phones visible on desk will perform worse on working memory tasks than those with phones absent.

Method

N=30; between-subjects; three conditions: phone on desk, phone in bag, phone in another room. Digit span and N-back tasks.

Results

Data collection ongoing (n=18 so far). Preliminary results show a trend in predicted direction but significance not yet reached.

Conclusion

Pending completion. Expected Q3 2025. Interim findings reported in an accompanying essay.

The Loneliness Epidemic Is Also a Brain Epidemic

Chronic loneliness does not merely feel painful — it physically reshapes the brain. Elevated cortisol, reduced hippocampal volume, hyperactive threat-detection: the neuroscience of social isolation maps onto a public health crisis that receives nowhere near enough structural attention. As rates of loneliness reach historic highs across age groups, we should ask whether our built environments, digital habits, and institutional structures are neurologically viable.
